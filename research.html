Works in Progress


Increasing the Reach of Promising Individualized Academic Supports: Experimental Evidence of a Lower-Cost Technology-Infused Intervention (with Monica Bhatt, Jonathan Guryan, and Salman Khan)

Recent research has demonstrated that individualized, high-intensity daily math tutoring program, delivered as a course during the school day, can substantially improve outcomes for youth (Cook et al., 2015; Guryan et al., 2021). However, such interventions are prohibitively expensive for many school districts to bear at scale. This project explores whether a lower-cost version of this intervention - which reduces program cost by nearly half by replacing some in-person instruction with an individualized education technology platform - remains effective, based on results from a randomized controlled trial of the intervention across several Chicago and New York City public high schools. Preliminary findings suggest that this blended-learning model preserves the positive effects of high-dosage, in-person tutoring while reducing its cost – resulting in improved standardized math test scores and grades in math courses as well as reductions in math course failures. These preliminary results imply that this lower-cost blended-learning model has the potential to scale up in school districts across the country, to ensure that more students have the personalized support they need to succeed.



The Short and Long-Term Effects of the Thinking for a Change Program for Incarcerated Individuals (with Zachary Goodman).

The “Thinking for a Change” cognitive behavioral therapy program is provided to a substantial number of incarcerated individuals across state and federal prisons in the United States. However, no causal evidence exists about it’s effects. This work leverages several discontinuities in program eligibility in Pennsylvania and Idaho prisons to evaluate the causal effects of the T4C program, and how those effects differ across different types of incarcerated individuals and different margins of program eligibility.



Neighborhood Beautification and Crime – The Effects of the Toronto Street Arts Initiative (with Katherine DeCelles and Nicola Lacerta).

The Toronto Street Arts Initiative is a series of murals and art instillations painted and installed throughout Toronto over the past decade. How did these beautification efforts impact the city? This work examines the effect of each individual art installation on local crime, using a difference in differences framework that leverages the quasi-random timing of launch and location of each art project.



Temporal Bias in Officer Arrest and Use of Force Decisions – Evidence from Dallas

Prior work has shown differences in arrest and use of force propensity across officers in the same police department. This work investigates the extent to which individual officers show differences in arrest and use of force propensity from one arrest to the next, based on the prior crimes the officer responded to that day, the time already spent on shift that day, and other exogenous factors impacting the officer at the time of the call. I then discuss the extent to which these within-officer differences can be used as an instrument to evaluate the long-term effects of arrests and use of force incidents on potential arrestees.



The Effects of Mandatory Minimum Sentence Laws on Crime, Incarceration, and Plea Bargaining

I estimate the effects of U.S. mandatory minimum sentence laws on crime rates, incarceration rates, and plea bargaining rates using a difference-in-differences estimation strategy. First, I compile all state and national mandatory minimum law changes over the past 25 years, with a particular focus on mandatory minimum sentence laws relating to drug crimes (marijuana, cocaine, and heroine). I use plea bargaining data from the Bureau of Justice Statistics’ (BJS) State Court Processing (SCP) statistics, which covers 40 of the most populous counties in the United States, located across 25 states. I use incarceration data from the BJS’s National Prisoner Statistics (NPS) program, and crime data from the Uniform Crime Reporting (UCR) program and the National Incidence-Based Reporting System (NIBRS). Complied mandatory minimum law changes show that, over time, southern states have had the harshest mandatory minimum sentence laws followed by states in the upper Midwest, while states in the west and lower Midwest have had the most lenient mandatory minimum sentence laws. Additionally, compiled mandatory minimum law changes show that there has been substantial number of minor and moderate changes in state mandatory minimum sentence laws over the past 25 years, both in the crimes that carry mandatory minimum sentences and in the sentences for those crimes. Preliminary estimation results are forthcoming.



Estimating the Effects of High School Career and Technical Education over the Last Fifty Years and Predicting Future Effects

Using the dynamic structural model developed in LaForest (2022), I estimate and compare the effects of U.S. high school career and technical education (CTE) in the 1970s, 1980s, 1990s, and 2000s. I do so by estimating the parameters of the dynamic structural model using restricted-use data from each of the four panel data sets conducted by the National Center for Education Studies: the National Longitudinal Study of the High School Class of 1972 (NLS-72), High School and Beyond (HS&B), the National Education Longitudinal Study of 1988 (NELS:88), and the Education Longitudinal Study of 2002 (ELS:2002). I compare these results in order to provide context on how the returns to high school CTE have changed over time in the United States, to predict how the returns to high school CTE will change in the future, and to evaluate the robustness of my parameter estimates over time. Finally, I test how well my model predicts the data by treating ELS:2002 as a hold-out sample and using the trends in the NLS-72, HS&B, and NELS:88 model estimates to predict the data in ELS:2002. A preliminary comparison of results between the ELS:2002 and NELS:88 data sets suggests that the effects of high school CTE on later-life wages and chances of employment in a skilled occupation remained relatively constant between the 1990s and 2000s, but that the effects of trade CTE on overall employment decreased from positive to negligible between the 1990s and 2000s.



Wald Test for Common Mean Probabilities across Geographic Areas (with Steven Stern)

We show how one can construct a Wald test, to test for variation in estimates across locations, in the presence of small area-specific random effects which have a non-linear covariance structure. The methodology is applied to estimates of mental illness prevalence for small areas in Virginia described in more detail in Johnson et al. (2016).
